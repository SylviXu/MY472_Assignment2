Exercise 2

Here I will visualise on a world map the extent of stay-at-home restriction policies in different regions in 2020-2022 and the income support index for the corresponding period.

I use different shades of red fills to indicate the stringency of the stay-at-home policies in different regions, with darker colours illustrating more restrictive policies for the region. On the other hand, I project yellow bubbles onto the map to indicate the income support index for each area, with higher indexes resulting in larger bubbles.

As we can see from the graph, mainland China, which has the darkest colour, consistently implemented the most restrictive stay-at-home policies from 2020 to 2022, with residents more likely to be asked not to leave their houses unless it was essential to do so. The American Continent is generally more balanced, with a slight tightening of restriction in 2021 compared to 2020, but an overall loosening in 2022, with the same lighter red colour as the rest of the continent except for mainland China. This feature indicates that the level of restriction is approaching zero and that the vast majority of residents will be able to travel freely across the region. The overall situation in the Southeast Asia and Africa region is similar to that of the American Continent, where the level of restriction in the policy is first tightened and then liberalised. However, the Europe and Oceania regions show a smooth downward trend in the degree of policy restriction over the three-year period, as the change in the shade of colour in these regions is relatively less pronounced, but generally getting lighter.

In terms of income support, overall the support provided in all regions of the world is positively correlated with the stringency of the stay-at-home policy. But theres still some specific information that worth noting. From observing the distribution of the bubbles, we can know that although the European region did not have a particularly stringent stay-at-home policy, a larger number of countries in Europe provided income subsidy support during the period, for the region has the highest density of bubbles as can be seen from the figure. North America has a higher number of large diameter bubbles located in the region, demonstrating the stronger income support offered by North American countries. In addition, the general support was also favourable in Asia, with both mainland China, which had the most strict policies, and the surrounding coastal regions supplying a higher level of income assistance (income support data are missing from the map for regions such as Russia, due to the fact that the names and codes of the countries in the source data do not partially correspond to the indicators supplied in the geonames package). Nevertheless, only a few countries in Africa gave income support during the pandemic, as the bubbles there were more sparsely distributed.

  #Select "E1" as the main indicator.

  filter(!is.na(Support_index))

#Create a function to get latitude and longitude data for national capitals.
get_capital_coordinates <- function(country_name) {
  country_code <- countrycode(country_name, origin = 'country.name', destination = 'iso3c')
  capital_info <- GNsearch(q = country_code, featureClass = "P", maxRows = 1)
  if (!is.null(capital_info$lat) && !is.null(capital_info$lng)) {
    return(data.frame(country = country_code, latitude = capital_info$lat, longitude = capital_info$lng))
  } else {
    return(NULL)
  }
}

#Use the function created above to extract the coordinates of the capitals of the countries in the dataset.
capital_coordinates <- lapply(Eco_support$CountryName, get_capital_coordinates)

#Research Question: What are murder crimes mentioned in The Sherlock Holmes novels?
#Load the quanteda library.
library(quanteda)

#Load the book files.
dir_path <- getwd()
book_files <- list.files(dir_path, pattern = "\\.txt$", full.names = TRUE)

#Store text data.
a_study_in_scarlet <- paste(readLines("A Study In Scarlet.txt", warn = FALSE), collapse = "\n")
the_sign_of_the_four <- paste(readLines("The Sign of the Four.txt", warn = FALSE), collapse = "\n")
the_hound_of_the_baskervilles <- paste(readLines("The Hound of the Baskervilles.txt", warn = FALSE), collapse = "\n")
the_valley_of_fear <- paste(readLines("The Valley of Fear.txt", warn = FALSE), collapse = "\n")

my_texts <- c(a_study_in_scarlet, the_sign_of_the_four, the_hound_of_the_baskervilles, the_valley_of_fear)
names(my_texts) <- c("A Study In Scarlet", "The Sign of the Four", "The Hound of the Baskervilles", "The Valley of Fear")

#Build a corpus from the text data.
novel_corpus <- corpus(my_texts,
                       docvars = data.frame(name = names(my_texts),
                                            characters = str_count(my_texts))
)
#Preview the features of the corpus.
docvars(novel_corpus)

#Create a dictionary that contains terms of types of crime.
crime_dictionary <- dictionary(list(
  murder = c("*murder*", "attempted murder", "manslaughter", "homicid"),
  theft = c("theft","thief","thieves", "steal", "robbery"),
  blackmail = c("*blackmail*", "extort*"),
  kidnap = c("kidnap*", "abduct*", "imprison*"),
  other = c("treason", "disloyal*","counterfeit*")))


#Create a document-feature matrix.
dfmplain <- novel_corpus %>%
  tokens(remove_punct = TRUE) %>% 
  dfm()

#Count the frequency of words for each type of crime.
count_crime <- dfm_lookup(dfmplain, dictionary = crime_dictionary,
                             valuetype = "glob") %>%
  convert(to = "data.frame") %>%
  summarise(Murder = sum(murder),
            Theft = sum(theft),
            Blackmail = sum(blackmail),
            Kidnap = sum(kidnap),
            Other = sum(other))
